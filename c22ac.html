    How effective is prescribed burning in mitigating negative effects of wildfire on communities, while enhancing biodiversity?

    Khang Vo

    Updated 4:00 p.m. GTM-7, April 27th, 2024

    Wildfires pose a persistent challenge for policymakers, inflicting millions of dollars in damages to homes, forests, and sometimes even claiming lives annually. In recent decades, prescribed burning has emerged as a key strategy to mitigate wildfire risks. This method involves controlled burns in targeted areas to clear out flammable materials, thereby reducing fuel availability for large wildfires. These small fires can be extinguished easily if they become hostile. While prescribed burning has proven effective in wildfire prevention, concerns have arisen regarding its efficacy amidst the increasing frequency of high-intensity wildfires, likely influenced by climate change. Consequently, the primary objective of this research is to assess the effectiveness of prescribed burning under changing different factors. Furthermore, while prescribed burning serves to protect communities, it also raises environmental concerns including bad air quality, increased carbon emission, and erosion. Thus, the second objective is to explore the relationship between prescribed burning and the listed concerns. Additionally, public concerns have been raised about the potential negative impact of frequent burning on biodiversity. Therefore, the final objective of this study is to investigate the impact of prescribed burning on vegetation and animal populations. Ultimately, the goal of this research is to propose solutions for the development of effective fire control policies that prioritize both community and environmental sustainability.

    Frequent prescribed burning has raised concerns about potentially contaminating the air quality in local neighborhoods. The 2003 Lion Fire case study in the southern Sierra Nevada mountains provides insights into this issue. This fire covered over 33,000 acres and produced substantial smoke emissions that were carried long distances by wind patterns to the San Joaquin Valley and surrounding foothill communities in central California. The authors state that "the smoke was injected into a region that included several densely populated cities" (Schweizer and Cisneros 2014, p. 268). This contaminated the air breathed by millions of residents. However, the authors' detailed measurements and analysis showed that while smoke was present, the concentration of harmful particulate matter (PM2.5) in the air remained below the National Ambient Air Quality Standard set by the EPA, which is considered safe for public health.

    Specifically, they report that "maximum 24-hour PM2.5 levels remained below 65 μg/m3 [...] well below the federal standard of 35 μg/m3" (p. 274). Despite these reassuring results showing pollution levels within acceptable thresholds, the authors recommend fire managers take extra precautions for sensitive groups like the elderly, children, and those with respiratory issues who may experience impacts at lower PM2.5 levels. Their advice includes conducting prescribed burns only on low wind speed days and during humid conditions to minimize smoke drift into populated areas that could affect these vulnerable groups. Additionally, they suggest burning techniques like multi-day ignitions to reduce extreme spikes in PM2.5 levels. With such proactive planning and mitigation tactics, the authors conclude that prescribed burning operations like the Lion Fire "can be conducted with minimal air quality impacts" (p. 277) while allowing the important ecological benefits of prescribed fire. Their multi-year data provides evidence that frequent burning need not contaminate neighborhood air quality when properly managed.

    Increasing burning frequency also raised public concern about releasing a harming amount of carbon in the atmosphere, which worsen the affect of climate change. However, a research showed the opposite of this concern. Prescribed burning is an increasingly utilized approach for reducing overall forest carbon emissions to the atmosphere. While it is true that prescribed fires do release carbon monoxide, carbon dioxide, and other greenhouse gases during the controlled combustion process, the research provides strong evidence that the emissions are significantly less than those from uncontrolled, high-severity wildfires. As stated in the study by Wiedinmyer and Hurteau, "The carbon emissions from prescribed burning were three to six times lower per unit area burned than wildfires" (p. 1930). Their modeling analyzed the effects across several forest types and found prescribed fire yielded these emission reduction benefits consistently across ponderosa pine, Douglas fir, and mixed conifer forests in the western United States.

    However, the researchers emphasized that a variety of influence factors can impact the degree of emission reductions, such as frequency and spatial distribution of the prescribed burn areas, the specific forest type and structure, and the amount of available fuel loads. Their data indicated the emission reduction benefits were greatest in forests with higher fuel loads compared to lower-load forests. Additionally, they found that increasing prescribed burn frequencies up to a certain point can substantially lower emissions over time as fuel loads are kept lower. As the authors summarize, "Widespread reintroduction of prescribed burning in fire-adapted forests of the western United States has the potential to substantially reduce wildfire emissions and their detrimental impacts on air quality" (p. 1931). By proactively managing forests with the strategic use of controlled prescribed burning, Wiedinmyer and Hurteau's research provides quantitative evidence that this approach can be an effective tool for lowering overall carbon emissions relative to the more catastrophic emission levels produced by severe wildfires.

    Burning destroyed the vegetation and habitats of various species. Many people worried that prescribed burning would kill animals and greatly reduced the biodiversity. Nonetheless, according to "Prescribed Burning vs. Wildfire Impacts on Plants, Lizards, and Mammals" by Pastro, he indicated that this method have positive impacts on plant populations compared to wildfires. Prescribed burning removes weeds, parasitic plants, and understory growth while leaving larger overstory plants relatively unharmed. This lowers competition for resources like light, water, and nutrients, promoting the overall environmental health of the ecosystem. Prescribed fires also give an advantage to fire-adapted, pyrophytic plant species that are better able to survive and regenerate after low-intensity burns. This increases local biodiversity while reducing fuel loads that could feed high-intensity wildfires (Pastro et al. 2011). In contrast, uncontrolled wildfires are often highly destructive to vegetation across all strata. While fast-growing weeds and invasive species can recover quickly post-fire, the taller overstory plants take much longer to re-establish, allowing weedy species to outcompete them and hinder forest regeneration. Thus, wildfires tend to decrease biodiversity compared to prescribed burning practices.

    Furthermore, more positive impacts were observed on animal populations compared to wildfire effects. The lower intensity and controlled application of prescribed fire allows mobile animals like mammals and birds to safely flee the burn area to unburned refuges nearby (Pastro et al. 2011). It also provides a rejuvenating ecological disturbance that can benefit certain taxa. For example, prescribed burning has been shown to increase abundances of invertebrates like insects and arachnids in the years post-burn, which attracts and increases populations of insectivorous species like lizards and small mammals that rely on these food sources (Valkó et al. 2016). Larger mammals are typically not impacted as they can easily escape localized prescribed fire fronts.

    In contrast, the rapid speed and high-intensity heat of wildfires can be devastating for animal populations. Fire intensities often prevent buffering species loss by leaving few unburned refuges nearby for wildlife to escape. The geographic extent and severity of wildfires mean they are much more likely to cause direct mortality to less mobile species (Pastro et al. 2011). This combined with the long-term habitat destruction results in major losses of biodiversity across plant and animal communities after wildfires compared to prescribed burns.

    While prescribed burning provided multiple benefit, it also had some negative impacts on the evironments. Fire managers should take into consideration the risk of soil erosion when initiating prescribed burning operations. The study by Morris et al. (2014) highlights this issue, finding that sediment yield increased by up to 700% and runoff increased by up to 200% in the first year after prescribed burning in the Mount Lofty Ranges of Australia. This occurs because as the vegetation and ground cover is burned off and plant roots die, the soil loses the stabilizing ability of those root systems to hold it in place against the erosive forces of water penetration and flooding events. Without this protection, the recently burned areas are highly susceptible to excessive erosion and soil loss until new vegetation can become re-established. The researchers emphasize the importance of conducting soil testing and developing erosion mitigation plans as part of the prescribed burn management process. This could include strategies like installing erosion barriers, avoiding burning on steep slopes, maintaining unburned patches, and timing burns based on predicted rainfall to reduce the erosion risk window. While prescribed burning provides ecological benefits, Morris et al. clearly demonstrate fire managers must carefully evaluate and plan for potential soil erosion impacts as well.

    Under the effect of modern climate changes, it is harder to conduct this operation. One of the primary challenges facing prescribed burning is the narrowing window of opportunity for conducting these controlled burns safely and effectively. With rising temperatures and increasingly arid landscapes, the conditions suitable for prescribed burning are becoming more elusive. The 2019-2020 Australian bushfires serve as a sobering testament to this reality. Despite extensive prescribed burning efforts, these fires raged with unprecedented ferocity, fueled by weather conditions that were remarkably hotter and drier than in previous years. Experimental simulations conducted by researchers like Hamish Clark and his team have corroborated these findings, predicting that as average temperatures continue to climb, more extreme fire weather events will become commonplace, significantly diminishing the effectiveness of prescribed burning as a standalone strategy.

    Compounding this challenge is the impact of rising greenhouse gas emissions on the available burning window. Climate models project that under high-emissions scenarios, the average number of days suitable for prescribed burning could plummet by 20-30% by the mid-21st century across vast swaths of the United States, including the Appalachian Mountains, where declines of 40% or more are anticipated. These projections are rooted in the reality that increased greenhouse gas concentrations amplify the frequency and severity of extreme conditions, such as soaring temperatures, low humidity, and diminished precipitation – factors that collectively curtail the viability of prescribed burning operations.

    In regions already grappling with the consequences of climate change, the prognosis is even more concerning. Simulations conducted under the RCP 8.5 scenario, which envisions a world characterized by high greenhouse gas concentrations and an average temperature increase of 3.7°C by the end of the 21st century, paint a dire picture for Mediterranean landscapes. These areas are projected to become increasingly hot, dry, and fire-prone, effectively shrinking the burning window to a mere sliver of the year when conditions are suitable for prescribed fires.

    Faced with these mounting challenges, researchers and fire managers are advocating for a multifaceted approach that integrates prescribed burning with complementary strategies. Forest thinning, a practice that involves selectively removing trees and vegetation to reduce fuel loads, is increasingly being viewed as a crucial component of a comprehensive fire management plan. By reducing the density of combustible materials, forest thinning can enhance the effectiveness of prescribed burning while mitigating the risk of high-severity wildfires. Additionally, researchers emphasize the importance of community-level fire prevention efforts, such as creating defensible spaces around homes and implementing stringent building codes to fortify structures against ember attacks.

    Ultimately, prescribed burning is a effective method in managing wildfire, carbon emission, and faciliating biodiversity. However, as the challenges posed by climate change and environmental concerns continue to mount, it is clear that a one-size-fits-all approach to fire management is no longer tenable. Fire managers and policy makers, must integrate strategy that accounts for the unique characteristics of each landscape and the specific risks and vulnerabilities it faces.
